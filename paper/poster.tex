\block{The Problem in One Slide}{
  \textbf{You fit a regression on the full dataset.} The coefficients look reasonable, $R^2$ is decent, p-values are small.
  A reviewer asks: \textit{``Does this hold for men and women separately? For young and old? For each site?''}

  \vspace{4pt}
  \begin{center}
    \textbf{You check. It doesn't. Now what?}
  \end{center}
}

\block{Study Design: Four Models Compared}{
  \small
  For each dataset or simulated replicate we fit four nested specifications:

  \vspace{4pt}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}cl@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Specification} \\
    \midrule
    M1 (Pooled)     & $Y = \beta_0 + X^\top\beta + \varepsilon$ \\
    M2 (Intercept)  & $Y = \beta_0 + X^\top\beta + \gamma G + \varepsilon$ \\
    M3 (Interaction)& $Y = \beta_0 + X^\top\beta + \gamma G + X^\top\delta G + \varepsilon$ \\
    M4 (Separate)   & $Y = \beta_{0,g} + X^\top\beta_g + \varepsilon$ \quad (per group) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}

  \vspace{6pt}
  This progression isolates: (i) intercept shift, (ii) slope heterogeneity, (iii) full subgroup structure.
}

\block{Five Stability Metrics}{
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.20\linewidth} p{0.34\linewidth} p{0.40\linewidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Definition} & \textbf{What it captures} \\
    \midrule
    Sign Stability Rate (SSR)
    & $\mathrm{SSR}_j = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m} \mathbf{1}(\mathrm{sgn}\,\hat\beta_{j,a_m} = \mathrm{sgn}\,\hat\beta_{j,b_m})$
    & Do substantive conclusions ($+$/$-$) reverse across subgroups? \\[8pt]

    Standardized Drift
    & $D_j^\star = \frac{\max_g|\hat\beta_{j,g} - \hat\beta_{j,\text{pooled}}|}{\widehat{\text{SE}}(\hat\beta_{j,\text{pooled}})}$
    & How far do subgroup coefficients move from pooled, in SE units? \\[8pt]

    Context-Normalized Drift
    & $\widetilde{D}_j = \dfrac{D_j^\star}{1 + M_j}$,\newline
      $M_j = \frac{1}{\binom{G}{2}}\sum_{a<b}\frac{|\bar{x}_{j,a}-\bar{x}_{j,b}|}{s_{j,\text{pooled}}}$
    & Drift after accounting for covariate distribution differences. \textbf{The key diagnostic.} \\[8pt]

    Transported Prediction Gap
    & $\mathrm{TPG}_{a\to b} = \mathrm{RMSE}_{\text{pooled}\to b} - \mathrm{RMSE}_{b\to b}$
    & How much prediction error increases when a model travels across groups. \\[8pt]

    Covariate Shift Summary
    & $\mathrm{CSS}_{a,b} = \frac{1}{p}\sum_{j}\frac{|\bar{x}_{j,a} - \bar{x}_{j,b}|}{s_{j,\text{pooled}}}$
    & How different are the $X$ distributions between groups? \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
}

\block{The Key Decomposition: Why Context-Normalized Drift Is Smaller}{
  \small
  Under OLS, even with \textbf{identical} true $\beta$ across groups:
  \[
    \hat\beta^{(g)} - \hat\beta^{(\text{pooled})} = (X_g'X_g)^{-1}X_g'\varepsilon_g - (X'X)^{-1}X'\varepsilon
  \]
  The variance: $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta^{(g)} - \hat\beta^{(\text{pooled})}) = \sigma^2[(X_g'X_g)^{-1} + (X'X)^{-1}]$.

  When group $g$ has a different $X$ distribution, $(X_g'X_g)^{-1} \neq (X'X)^{-1}$, and the difference inflates \textbf{even when $\beta$ is identical}. This is the \textbf{mechanical} component of drift.

  \vspace{6pt}
  \begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
      \node[draw=DarkBlue, fill=DarkBlue!5, rounded corners=6pt,
            inner sep=10pt, text width=0.92\linewidth, align=center,
            font=\large\bfseries] {
        $\displaystyle
        \underbrace{D_j^\star}_{\text{Total observed drift}}
        \;=\;
        \underbrace{\widetilde{D}_j}_{\text{Residual (unexplained by $X$)}}
        \;\times\;
        \underbrace{(1 + M_j)}_{\text{Mechanical inflation from covariate spread}}
        $
      };
    \end{tikzpicture}
  \end{center}

  \vspace{4pt}
  \textbf{Three diagnostic regimes:}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item[$M_j\approx 0$:] $\widetilde{D}_j \approx D_j^\star$. No correction needed. All drift is ``real.''
    \item[$M_j$ large, $\widetilde{D}_j$ small:] Drift is an \textbf{artifact} of different $X$ distributions. Reassuring.
    \item[$M_j$ large, $\widetilde{D}_j$ still large:] Genuine parameter heterogeneity, confirmed after correction.
  \end{itemize}
}

  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Drift is driven by $G$ and per-group $n$, not total $N$.

\block{Take-Home Messages}{
  \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=6pt]
    \item \textbf{Regression conclusions are fragile.}
          Subgroup-specific estimates routinely disagree with pooled estimates in direction, magnitude, and significance.

    \item \textbf{Not all drift is real.}
          Covariate distribution differences across groups mechanically inflate coefficient differences. Context-normalized drift separates artifact from genuine instability.

    \item \textbf{Simpson's paradox is not a curiosity.}
          It is a special case of a general phenomenon: pooling across heterogeneous subgroups produces misleading estimates.

    \item \textbf{Diagnose before you conclude.}
          Before reporting subgroup results, compute: SSR (are signs stable?), $D_j^\star$ (how far do coefficients move?), $\widetilde{D}_j$ (is it real or artifact?), TPG (does prediction degrade across groups?).

    \item \textbf{The five-metric framework}
          provides a systematic, reproducible way to assess regression stability beyond significance testing.
  \end{enumerate}
