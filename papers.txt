Assumption-Lean Inference for Regression: Buja et al. (2024)
confronts the instability of standard inference when a linear model is a convenient approximation rather than a true generative model. The paper shows how standard errors under model violations can be dramatically different from conventional ones, potentially leading to "the breakdown of the classical ancillarity argument for predictors."

How to use and summary:
Summary of the paper:
Berk, Buja, Brown, George, Kuchibhotla, Su, and Zhao (2018) argue that standard regression models are almost always misspecified, and instead of pretending they are correct, we should treat OLS coefficients as best linear approximations to the true conditional mean function. Under this “assumption‑lean” view, the only requirements are i.i.d. sampling from a joint distribution of (Y,X); no linearity, homoskedasticity, or normality is assumed. The population coefficients minimize expected squared error, and the usual OLS estimates remain asymptotically unbiased and normal for that target. However, because the coefficients depend on the marginal distribution of the regressors (through the optimal approximation), they are not ancillary: if the X distribution shifts, the estimand itself changes. The paper provides sandwich and x-y bootstrap standard errors that are valid under misspecification, shows that predicted values at fixed x are biased (the bias does not vanish with sample size), but that prediction intervals can be calibrated on the training data to achieve correct coverage. It also offers a novel interpretation of slopes as distance‑weighted averages of pairwise slopes.
Where and how to use it in your paper:
In your introduction and literature review, cite this paper to justify your core premise: pooled regression coefficients are not “true” parameters but approximations that depend on the distribution of predictors. This frames your entire investigation of sign stability, coefficient drift, and transportability.
In your methods section (covariate shift), refer to their Figure 2 (and surrounding discussion) to explain why the best linear approximation changes when the covariate distribution differs between subgroups – this is the theoretical reason your “transported prediction gap” and “standardized coefficient drift” can occur even if the underlying response surface is the same.
In your simulation design, note that you treat regressors as random and drawn from subgroup‑specific distributions, aligning with their recommendation not to condition on regressors as fixed. You can also compare conventional standard errors to sandwich or bootstrap estimates in your results to show how misspecification affects inference.
In your discussion, use their conclusion that “regressor variability must be included in treatments of the sampling distribution” to argue that analysts should always check subgroup differences in predictors when interpreting pooled coefficients. Their warning about causal inference under misspecification also helps you delimit the scope of your claims – you are not estimating causal effects, only describing associations that may not transport.
